# Assignment 1. What is something you believe to be true that most people around you would disagree with?

### Rules

* No "safe" answers.
* Must be your real belief (not copied from internet).
* Minimum 50 words.

**Hint:** What do you believe about career, money, learning, discipline, relationships, health, success, life, tech industry, etc. that most people don't agree with?

## Answer

I believe that someone who is consistent, always seeks to improve, and remains visible will eventually meet their luck. Consistency beats talent almost every time. While many people around me rely on natural gifts or wait for the perfect opportunity, I’ve observed that those who commit to daily learning and steady progress ultimately outperform them. Small improvements compound into remarkable results over time. This belief has transformed my approach to mastering DevOps. Instead of chasing shortcuts, I focus on building strong habits, completing real projects, and making my work visible. I’m convinced that discipline creates opportunities that motivation and luck alone rarely can.

# Assignment 2. What are the top 3 objective truths you discovered through experimentation and results?

### Definition

Objective truths do not depend on opinions. They hold true regardless of how people feel.

Write each truth in this format:

**Truth:** (1 sentence)

**Evidence from my life:** (2–4 lines: what you tried + what happened)

---

## Truth #1

### Truth

You cannot improve a skill without consistent practice. Small improvements in work and learning compound over time.

### Evidence from my life

I have been learning cloud technologies for the past 3–5 years. Although I often felt there was no visible improvement day by day, when I look back I see significant progress in my knowledge and confidence. Whenever I practiced consistently for several days, I became noticeably more skilled and efficient. When I stopped for weeks, I forgot details and had to relearn them. This has taught me that consistency always beats occasional intense efforts.

---

## Truth #2

### Truth

When in doubt, reach out to people. You accelerate your growth faster by connecting with others than by struggling alone.

### Evidence from my life

I have discovered that people are generally willing to help if you have the courage to ask. By reaching out to professionals I admire or want to learn from, I’ve gained valuable advice, feedback, and sometimes even mentorship. The more I practiced reaching out in a respectful and professional way, the easier and more natural it became. This habit has helped me overcome obstacles quicker and move closer to my professional goals in FinOps, DevOps and cloud computing.

---

## Truth #3

### Truth

You can spend a lot of time reading, watching videos, and collecting certificates, but real knowledge and deep understanding come from hands-on experience and troubleshooting.

### Evidence from my life

Earlier in my career, I focused heavily on acquiring certificates and theoretical learning. Over time, I realized that implementing projects, running into errors, and troubleshooting them helped me remember concepts better and truly understand how systems work. The lessons I learned while fixing real problems stuck with me far longer than anything I had only read or studied. This shift from passive learning to active building has greatly improved my skills in DevOps and cloud technologies.

## Answer

I felt a deep sense of guilt whenever I tried to jump steps or take shortcuts in my learning, especially in cloud technologies. I realized that having weak fundamentals always comes back to haunt me later. Instead of properly understanding core concepts, I would rush through topics or chase certificates without enough hands-on practice.
This shortcut behavior made me feel guilty because I knew I was cheating myself. Deep down, I understood that skipping the fundamentals would create gaps in my knowledge that would slow me down in the future. The guilt pushed me to reflect and change my approach. Now I focus on building strong foundations in cloud and DevOps, even if it takes longer. The discomfort of guilt has taught me that real growth comes from consistent, honest effort rather than quick wins
